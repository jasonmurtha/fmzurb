General

  1. What is affordable housing preservation?

    Preservation of affordable homeownership entails ensuring that the price of a home is affordable to the initial buyer and subsequent buyers over a long-term period, whether purchasing a newly constructed home or an existing home. Additionally, preserving affordable housing can also be achieved by reducing housing costs through utility savings, and through energy efficiency home improvements.

  2. What are Freddie Mac's Single-Family Duty to Serve activities for affordable housing preservation?

    Through our Duty to Serve initiatives, we’re:

    • Facilitating financing of energy efficiency improvements through first lien mortgages
    • Testing and optimizing innovative product features and underwriting guidelines through pilot programs
    • Developing guidelines to facilitate new mortgage originations for properties in community land trusts and other shared equity programs
    • Conducting research on energy efficiency and shared equity markets, to better align our offerings with these markets’ needs
    • Providing borrower education and resources for lenders about Freddie Mac shared equity offering and energy efficiency financing options

Energy Efficiency

  1. What are Freddie Mac's Single-Family activities for energy efficiency improvements on first-lien single-family homes to support affordable housing?

    Freddie Mac supports the energy efficiency market, and through our Duty to Serve initiatives, we’re:

    • Conducting research on the impact of energy efficiency improvements on property values and loan mortgage performance and publish our research findings in 2019.
    • Developing valuation guidance and data collection mechanisms to account for energy-efficient features
    • Designing new or improved product flexibilities
    • Purchasing mortgages on properties with energy efficiency features
    • Allowing lenders to use energy efficiency to compensate for higher debt-to-income ratios
    • Permitting cash-out refinance transactions that allow borrowers to
      • make energy efficiency improvements
      • pay off existing debt obligations related to energy efficiency improvements
      • pay off property assessed clean energy (PACE) loans
  2. What is the greatest challenge lenders face in the energy efficiency market?

    The greatest challenge is that there is currently no standardized way in which energy efficiency improvements are capitalized into home values. Therefore, borrowers are not able to consistently realize the value of their investment in energy efficiency in their homes.

  3. Do you have any loan products available around energy efficiency improvements?

    Freddie Mac does not have a specific product to finance energy efficiency improvements. Instead we provide flexibilities to finance energy efficiency improvements through any eligible product in the Freddie Mace Single-Family Seller/Servicer Guide (Guide). We do this because we received market feedback that niche products do not work as intended and it was recommended to Freddie Mac by market participants that we offer product flexibilities that can be applied to all Guide eligible products. Please access our energy conservation improvement fact sheet for more information.

    Please note: We purchase mortgages secured by properties with such improvements through any mortgage product and property type eligible under our Guide.

Shared Equity

  1. What is Freddie Mac doing to support affordable housing for shared equity?

    Through our Duty to Serve initiatives, we’re:

    • Leveraging relationships with local organizations to broaden access to homebuyer education and housing
    • Develop guidelines to facilitate new mortgage originations for properties in community land trusts and properties with income-based resale restrictions.
    • Conducting research on shared equity programs to better understand this market and identify new opportunities to support it
    • Buying loans originated under shared equity programs to increase liquidity
    • Supporting standardization of data collection at the transaction level
    • Promoting market awareness of shared equity programs
  2. What are shared-equity homeownership programs under Freddie Mac's Single-Family Duty to Serve?

    Under Duty to Serve, shared equity homeownership programs provide homeownership opportunities with lasting or long-term affordability. These programs generally make a one-time investment to create affordable homeownership for homebuyers with low or moderate income. In return, the homeowners agree to certain restrictions, including limitations on returns upon resale or limitations on conveyances of the property. In effect, homeowners “share” some of the proceeds from resale to pay the opportunity forward to the next homebuyer with low or moderate income.

    Most of these programs are resale-restricted programs, which means that they restrict the home’s maximum selling price and who may purchase the property to income-eligible households. Hence, buyers with low or moderate income purchase and resell homes at prices below fair market value, to keep the home affordable.

    The greatest challenge is that there is currently no standardized way in which energy efficiency improvements are capitalized into home values. Therefore, borrowers are not able to consistently realize the value of their investment in energy efficiency in their homes.

  4. What's a community land trust?

    A community land trust (CLT) is a type of permanently affordable homeownership program. Under the Community Land Trust model, the Community Land Trust owns land and leases it under a long-term ground lease to low- and moderate-income homebuyers who finance the purchase of the structure on the land through a first lien mortgage. The ground lease contains certain land use restrictions and other restrictions such as occupancy, resale price and financing that ensure the continued use of the property for low-  and moderate-income households.

    Community land trusts (CLTs) are typically community-based nonprofits, state or local governments. They typically acquire properties by purchasing them at market rate or at a discount, through in-kind donations, or via state/local agencies. The borrower must sign a ground lease.

  5. What's a community land trust ground lease?

    The vast majority of CLTs utilize renewable, 99-year ground leases to create shared equity homeownership opportunities. The homeowner leases the land from the CLT for a nominal monthly ground lease fee while s/he owns and obtains mortgage financing for the improvements. Sometimes, the CLT provides additional subsidy beyond the fair market value of the land in order to make the home affordable for a household with low or moderate income.

  6. What is an income-based resale restricted program?

     Income-based resale restricted programs, also known as deed restriction programs, are a type of permanently affordable homeownership program that create and preserve affordable housing for borrowers with low and moderate income. These programs restrict the sales price of properties for initial and subsequent borrowers. The restrictions are imposed by nonprofits, state and local governments or municipalities, and are recorded prescribed and enforced through a legal document called a deed covenant.

    Deed covenants sometimes only have 30-year terms due to state rules against perpetuities. In these instances, affordability of the homes is preserved by ensuring the shared equity program has the preemptive option to purchase the home and may transfer this option to an eligible buyer. Income-based resale restricted programs have every new homeowner sign a new deed covenant with a new term. That way, the affordable home is preserved and serves subsequent homebuyers with low or moderate income.

  7. I'm a lender, why should I participate in shared equity programs?

    In addition to strengthening your commitment to help more families with very low-, low- and moderate-income attain and sustain homeownership, here are other reasons to consider shared equity programs:

    • May help you meet your Community Reinvestment Act (CRA) mandates – based on your CRA strategy.
    • Increase your affordable footprint – provides entry into a niche market and help you strengthen your affordability goals
    • Expand business opportunities – These programs are not markets that are served. If borrowers are trying to capture a new market within affordable space, this could be something that they want to consider.

    Freddie Mac will buy mortgages secured by properties subject to income-based resale restrictions and properties in community land trusts so you will have access to liquidity in the secondary mortgage market. Freddie Mac believes that shared equity providers are entities that may help mitigate losses if a borrower is in default.  Studies show that shared equity homeowners were 10 times less likely to be in foreclosure than homeowners in the conventional market across all incomes.

  8. Does borrower receive any equity when home is sold?

    Not always. If the home has appreciated in value in accordance with the resale restriction formula, the borrower would share any equity accumulated on the property with the shared equity provider. The borrower agrees to share any equity gains on the property in exchange for having an opportunity to own a home. If the home did not appreciate in value, then the borrower may not get any equity during a sale of the property. Most shared equity programs are designed to ensure that the borrower is not walking away with less money than what they originally put into the initial home financing transaction.

    Please note: The term shared equity refers to the equity that the program administrator is sharing with the borrower in order for the home to be affordable. With CLTs, they share their equity position on the land with the borrower by allowing the borrower to use the land at a reduced lease fee. And, in the income-based resale restricted programs, the program administrator is sharing in their equity position in the property by allowing the borrower to purchase the home at a significantly subsidized price.

  9. What is the role that lenders play in shared equity lending initiatives?

    Lenders provide the first lien mortgage financing.

  10. What restrictions must a borrower agree to for a community land trust deed and restriction shared equity programs?

    Borrowers must agree to the following restrictions for community last trusts and income-based resale restricted programs:

    • Sales price: program prescribes limits on resale price of the property for initial and subsequent buyers.
    • Occupancy: most programs will require that the property be a primary home, owner-occupied home
    • Financing: the program will review any first or secondary financing that the borrower may obtain
    • Allowable property improvements: the program will review any property improvements that the borrower may want to do on the property.
